The Borough

Gateway to Southeast Alaska

The Ketchikan Gateway Borough encompasses Revillagigedo Island and surrounding areas at the southern tip of Alaska's Inside Passage. Founded as a salmon cannery site in 1885, Ketchikan has grown into a vibrant community where Tlingit, Haida, and Tsimshian traditions thrive alongside world-class fishing, arts, and adventure tourism.

Stretching 31 miles long but never more than 10 blocks wide, this island city hugs dramatic bluffs along the Tongass National Forest — the largest temperate rainforest in the world. Accessible only by air or sea, Ketchikan sits approximately 679 air miles north of Seattle and 90 miles north of Prince Rupert, British Columbia.

Downtown Ketchikan is designated a National Historic Landmark District, preserving the rich history of Alaska's southeastern frontier.

Heritage

Living Culture & Totem Traditions

The Tlingit name Kichx̱áan means "spread wings of a prostrate eagle" — a reference to the shape of the creek flowing through town.

Chief Kyan Pole

Located in Whale Park, this iconic totem is featured in most U.S. passports. Carved to honor Chief Kyan of the Tongass Tlingit.

Totem Heritage Center

Preserves 19th-century poles rescued from abandoned village sites. A living museum of Tlingit and Haida carving traditions.

Saxman Totem Park

Home to 25 totem poles, many recarvings of older poles created during the Civilian Conservation Corps era.

A Walk Through History

10,000+ BCE

First Peoples

Tlingit, Haida, and Tsimshian peoples establish fishing camps along Ketchikan Creek.

1885

Town Founded

Mike Martin establishes Clark & Martin saltery, founding the salmon canning industry.

1900

Gold Rush Era

Ketchikan becomes a supply hub for miners heading to the Klondike and other strikes.

1954

Creek Street Transforms

The famed red-light district closes; Creek Street becomes an arts and shopping destination.

1978

Misty Fjords Protected

Misty Fiords National Monument established — 3,570 square miles of wilderness.

Today

Living Community

A thriving arts scene, world-class fishing, and warm island hospitality welcome visitors year-round.

Getting Here

Fly into Ketchikan International Airport (KTN) with daily jet service from Seattle, Anchorage, and Juneau. The Alaska Marine Highway ferry system also connects Ketchikan to the Pacific Northwest.

🚢

Cruise Port

Four cruise ship berths welcome major cruise lines from May through September. Berth 2 houses the Visitor Information Center — your first stop for maps and tour bookings.

🎨

Arts & Culture

Explore galleries along Creek Street, catch a performance at the Saxman Community Hall, or visit the Southeast Alaska Discovery Center for interactive exhibits.

🎣

Outdoor Adventures

World-class sport fishing, kayaking through Misty Fjords, zip-lining through the canopy, and hiking trails for every skill level await outdoor enthusiasts.
